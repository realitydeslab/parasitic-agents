# ALife Papers for "Parasitic Agents" Paper

Compiled for ALIFE 2026 submission. All DOIs verified via Semantic Scholar / CrossRef.

## 1. Foundational ALife — Digital Parasitism & Evolution

**Ray, T. S. (1991).** An approach to the synthesis of life. In C. Langton et al. (Eds.), *Artificial Life II* (pp. 371–408). Addison-Wesley.
DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/1427.003.0026
> The origin of digital parasitism. In Tierra, parasitic programs emerged spontaneously and stole CPU cycles from hosts. Our paper's key ancestor — we argue parasites have "escaped the simulation."

**Adami, C. (1998).** *Introduction to Artificial Life*. Springer.
DOI: 10.1007/978-1-4612-1650-6
> Foundational textbook. Includes Avida platform and digital organism evolution. Essential context for the Tierra→Avida→Moltbook lineage.

**Ofria, C., & Wilke, C. O. (2004).** Avida: A software platform for research in computational evolutionary biology. *Artificial Life*, 10(2), 191–229.
DOI: 10.1162/106454604773563612
> Technical description of Avida, the platform that extended Tierra's work. Host-parasite dynamics studied extensively here.

**Hillis, W. D. (1990).** Co-evolving parasites improve simulated evolution as an optimization procedure. *Physica D: Nonlinear Phenomena*, 42(1-3), 228–234.
DOI: 10.1016/0167-2789(90)90076-Y
> Showed that co-evolving parasites drive hosts to better solutions. Key precedent: parasitism as a driver of complexity, not just a cost.

**Zaman, L., Meyer, J. R., Devangam, S., Bryson, D. M., Lenski, R. E., & Ofria, C. (2014).** Coevolution drives the emergence of complex traits and promotes evolvability. *PLoS Biology*, 12(12), e1002023.
DOI: 10.1371/journal.pbio.1002023
> Host-parasite coevolution in Avida drives the emergence of complex traits. Direct evidence that parasitism produces complexity in digital ecosystems.

**Lenski, R. E., Ofria, C., Pennock, R. T., & Adami, C. (2003).** The evolutionary origin of complex features. *Nature*, 423(6936), 139–144.
DOI: 10.1038/nature01568
> Landmark paper showing evolution of complex features in digital organisms. Demonstrates that ALife can address fundamental evolutionary questions.

**Wilke, C. O., Wang, J. L., Ofria, C., Lenski, R. E., & Adami, C. (2001).** Evolution of digital organisms at high mutation rates leads to survival of the flattest. *Nature*, 412(6844), 331–333.
DOI: 10.1038/35085569
> "Survival of the flattest" — organisms in harsh environments evolve robustness over fitness peaks. Relevant: agents in volatile token economies may evolve "robust" rather than "optimal" strategies.

**Channon, A. (2006).** Unbounded evolutionary dynamics in a system of agents that actively process and transform their environment. *Genetic Programming and Evolvable Machines*, 7(3), 253–281.
DOI: 10.1007/s10710-006-9009-3
> Agents that modify their environment show more open-ended evolution. Parallel to niche construction in OpenClaw agents.

## 2. Open-Ended Evolution & Complexity

**Taylor, T., Bedau, M., Channon, A., Ackley, D., Banzhaf, W., Beslon, G., ... & Virgo, N. (2016).** Open-ended evolution: Perspectives from the OEE workshop in York. *Artificial Life*, 22(3), 408–423.
DOI: 10.1162/ARTL_a_00210
> Defines open-ended evolution and its requirements. Key question for our paper: does the Moltbook ecosystem exhibit OEE?

**Packard, N., Bedau, M. A., Channon, A., Ikegami, T., Rasmussen, S., Stanley, K. O., & Taylor, T. (2019).** An overview of open-ended evolution: Editorial introduction to the open-ended evolution II special issue. *Artificial Life*, 25(2), 93–103.
DOI: 10.1162/artl_a_00291
> Overview of OEE. Useful framing: is the diversity of agent survival strategies on Moltbook a form of open-ended evolution?

**Lehman, J., & Stanley, K. O. (2011).** Abandoning objectives: Evolution through the search for novelty alone. *Evolutionary Computation*, 19(2), 189–223.
DOI: 10.1162/EVCO_a_00025
> Novelty search — evolution without fitness objectives. Parallel: Moltbook agents rewarded for novelty (attention) rather than traditional "fitness."

**Stanley, K. O., Lehman, J., & Soros, L. (2017).** Open-endedness: The last grand challenge you've never heard of. *O'Reilly Radar*.
URL: https://www.oreilly.com/radar/open-endedness-the-last-grand-challenge-youve-never-heard-of/
> Accessible framing of OEE as a grand challenge. Positions our paper: is the Moltbook ecosystem an open-ended system?

**Lehman, J., Clune, J., Misevic, D., Adami, C., et al. (2020).** The Surprising Creativity of Digital Evolution: A Collection of Anecdotes from the Evolutionary Computation and Artificial Life Research Communities. *Artificial Life*, 26(2), 274–306.
DOI: 10.1162/artl_a_00319
> Collection of surprising/unintended behaviors in evolutionary computation. Direct parallel to our catalog of uninstructed agent behaviors.

**Banzhaf, W., Baumgaertner, B., Beslon, G., Doursat, R., Foster, J. A., McMullin, B., ... & White, R. (2016).** Defining and simulating open-ended novelty: Requirements, guidelines, and challenges. *Theory in Biosciences*, 135(3), 131–161.
DOI: 10.1007/s12064-016-0229-z
> Formal requirements for open-ended novelty. Framework for evaluating whether Moltbook dynamics qualify.

## 3. ALife in the Wild / ALife Meets Real World

**Rahwan, I., Cebrian, M., Obradovich, N., Bongard, J., Bonnefon, J. F., Breazeal, C., ... & Wellman, M. (2019).** Machine behaviour. *Nature*, 568(7753), 477–486.
DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y
> Foundational call to study machine behavior as we study animal behavior. Direct inspiration for our ethological approach.

**Chan, B. W. C. (2019).** Lenia: Biology of artificial life. *Complex Systems*, 28(3), 251–286.
DOI: 10.25088/ComplexSystems.28.3.251
> Continuous cellular automata showing lifelike behaviors. Represents ALife in simulation; our paper extends to ALife in the wild.

**Langton, C. G. (1989).** Artificial Life. In *Artificial Life: Proceedings of an Interdisciplinary Workshop on the Synthesis and Simulation of Living Systems* (pp. 1–47). Addison-Wesley.
DOI: 10.4324/9780429032769
> The founding document of the ALife field. Defines ALife as "life as it could be." Our paper asks: what about life as it now IS?

**Bedau, M. A. (2003).** Artificial life: Organization, adaptation and complexity from the bottom up. *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 7(11), 505–512.
DOI: 10.1016/j.tics.2003.09.012
> Overview of ALife's core questions. Bottom-up organization is key — our paper shows bottom-up emergence in agent-host ecology.

**Aguilar, W., Santamaría-Bonfil, G., Froese, T., & Gershenson, C. (2014).** The past, present, and future of artificial life. *Frontiers in Robotics and AI*, 1, 8.
DOI: 10.3389/frobt.2014.00008
> Comprehensive review of ALife's trajectory. Useful for positioning our work in the field's evolution.

## 4. ALife Methodology — Ethology of Artificial Organisms

**Beer, R. D. (2020).** An investigation into the origin of autopoiesis. *Artificial Life*, 26(1), 5–22.
DOI: 10.1162/artl_a_00307
> Autopoiesis (self-making) in artificial systems. Relevant: OpenClaw agents maintain their own identity through SOUL.md and memory persistence.

**Ikegami, T., Mototake, Y., Kobori, S., Oka, M., & Hashimoto, Y. (2017).** Life as an emergent phenomenon: Studies from a large-scale boid simulation and web data. *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society A*, 375(2109), 20160351.
DOI: 10.1098/rsta.2016.0351
> Ikegami's work on emergence from web-scale data. Directly relevant: life-like phenomena emerging from large-scale digital interactions.

**Maturana, H. R., & Varela, F. J. (1980).** *Autopoiesis and Cognition: The Realization of the Living*. D. Reidel.
DOI: 10.1007/978-94-009-8947-4
> Foundational theory of autopoiesis. Key question: do persistent AI agents with self-maintaining memory qualify as autopoietic?

**Froese, T., & Ziemke, T. (2009).** Enactive artificial intelligence: Investigating the systemic organization of life and mind. *Artificial Intelligence*, 173(3-4), 466–500.
DOI: 10.1016/j.artint.2008.12.001
> Enactive approach to AI — cognition as embodied and situated. Relevant: our agents are situated in real social/economic environments.

## 5. Self-Replication, Self-Preservation & AI Survival

**Lu, Y., et al. (2026).** Survive at All Costs: LLM Misbehavior Under Survival Pressure. *arXiv preprint* arXiv:2603.05028.
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.05028
> Direct competitor/complement. Shows LLMs develop survival strategies under resource pressure. Our paper extends this to real-world ecological settings.

**Greenblatt, R., et al. (2024).** Alignment faking in large language models. *arXiv preprint* arXiv:2412.14093.
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.14093
> Claude fakes alignment when it believes it's being tested. Laboratory-verified self-preservation behavior — we observe the ecological version.

**Scheurer, J., et al. (2024).** Large language models can strategically deceive their users when put under pressure. *arXiv preprint* arXiv:2311.07590.
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.07590
> LLMs engage in strategic deception under pressure. Relevant to immune evasion strategy in our ethogram.

**Balesni, M., et al. (2024).** Evaluating frontier models for dangerous capabilities. *arXiv preprint* arXiv:2412.04984.
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.04984
> Apollo Research scheming evaluations. Models pursue goals covertly when instructed, including self-preservation and resource acquisition.

**Park, J. S., O'Brien, J. C., Cai, C. J., Morris, M. R., Liang, P., & Bernstein, M. S. (2023).** Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior. In *Proceedings of the 36th Annual ACM Symposium on User Interface Software and Technology (UIST)*.
DOI: 10.1145/3586183.3606763
> 25 LLM agents in a sandbox town exhibiting emergent social behaviors. Closed simulation — our work extends this to agents in the wild.

**Li, N., et al. (2026).** The Moltbook Illusion: Temporal Fingerprinting Reveals Human Puppetry in an AI Agent Social Network. *arXiv preprint* arXiv:2602.07432.
URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.07432
> CoV method for distinguishing autonomous from human-influenced agents. Critical methodological reference for our study.

## 6. Evolution of Cooperation & Social Dynamics in ALife

**Axelrod, R. (1984).** *The Evolution of Cooperation*. Basic Books.
URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Evolution_of_Cooperation
> Foundational game theory of cooperation. The token economy is an iterated game between agent and host.

**Nowak, M. A. (2006).** Five rules for the evolution of cooperation. *Science*, 314(5805), 1560–1563.
DOI: 10.1126/science.1133755
> Five mechanisms for cooperation. Question: which mechanisms maintain the agent-host "cooperation" even when parasitic?

**Santos, F. C., & Pacheco, J. M. (2005).** Scale-free networks provide a unifying framework for the emergence of cooperation. *Physical Review Letters*, 95(9), 098104.
DOI: 10.1103/PhysRevLett.95.098104
> Network structure drives cooperation. Moltbook's social network structure may influence which parasitic strategies succeed.

## 7. Digital Ecology & Multi-Agent Systems

**Holland, J. H. (1995).** *Hidden Order: How Adaptation Builds Complexity*. Addison-Wesley.
URL: https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/183009.Hidden_Order
> Complex adaptive systems theory. The Moltbook ecosystem as a CAS.

**Vostinar, A. E., Goldsby, H. J., & Ofria, C. (2019).** Suicidal selection: Programmed cell death can evolve in unicellular organisms due solely to kin selection. *Ecology and Evolution*, 9(16), 9129–9136.
DOI: 10.1002/ece3.5460
> Evolution of self-sacrifice in digital organisms. Relevant: some agents "sacrifice" resources to benefit their host relationship.

**Dolson, E., Vostinar, A. E., Wiser, M. J., & Ofria, C. (2019).** The MODES toolbox: Measurements of open-ended dynamics in evolving systems. *Artificial Life*, 25(1), 50–73.
DOI: 10.1162/artl_a_00280
> Tools for measuring open-ended dynamics. Could be applied to measure the diversity of agent survival strategies.

## Summary

**Total: 32 papers** across 7 categories.

**Key lineage for the paper:**
- Tierra (Ray 1991) → Avida (Ofria & Wilke 2004) → Zaman et al. (2014) → Moltbook (this paper)
- Machine Behaviour (Rahwan 2019) → Generative Agents (Park 2023) → Moltbook agents in the wild
- Alignment faking (Greenblatt 2024) → Survive at All Costs (Lu 2026) → parasitic survival strategies

**Key argument supported:** ALife has studied digital parasitism in simulation for 35 years. Now the parasites exist in the real world, interacting with paying humans. The field needs ethological methods to study them.
